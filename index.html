01 Boyle's Law

Discovered by Robert Boyle in 1662, this law relates the pressure and volume of a gas held at constant temperature.

STATEMENT The pressure of a fixed mass of gas at constant temperature is inversely proportional to its volume.
\( p \propto \dfrac{1}{V} \quad \Longrightarrow \quad pV = \text{constant} \quad \Longrightarrow \quad p_1 V_1 = p_2 V_2 \)

Plotting \(p\) against \(V\) gives a rectangular hyperbola. Plotting \(p\) against \(1/V\) gives a straight line through the origin — the classic linearisation test. Each curve at a different temperature is called an isothermal.

02 Charles' Law & the Pressure Law

Charles' Law holds pressure constant and relates volume to absolute temperature:

\( V \propto T \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \dfrac{V}{T} = \text{const} \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \dfrac{V_1}{T_1} = \dfrac{V_2}{T_2} \)

The Pressure Law holds volume constant and relates pressure to absolute temperature:

\( p \propto T \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \dfrac{p}{T} = \text{const} \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \dfrac{p_1}{T_1} = \dfrac{p_2}{T_2} \)
CRITICAL POINT Temperature must always be in Kelvin: \(T(\text{K}) = T(°\text{C}) + 273\). Zero on the Celsius scale is not zero molecular motion.
⬡ QUICK CHECK · Q2 · Short Answer
A gas occupies volume \(V\) at 127 °C. What temperature (in °C) gives volume 2.75V at constant pressure?
\(T_1 = 127 + 273 = 400\text{ K}\). Using \(V_1/T_1 = V_2/T_2\): \(T_2 = 400 \times 2.75 = 1100\text{ K} = 827°\text{C}\).
03 Kinetic Theory of Gases

The gas laws describe macroscopic behaviour (p, V, T). Kinetic theory provides the microscopic picture — molecules in rapid random motion — and connects the two scales.

Basic assumptions:

  • Large number of identical molecules in rapid, random motion.
  • Collisions between molecules and container walls are perfectly elastic (no kinetic energy lost).
  • Intermolecular forces are zero except during collisions.
  • Gravitational forces on molecules are negligible.
  • Total molecular volume is negligible compared to container volume.

04 Moles, Avogadro & Molecular Counting

One mole of any substance contains exactly \(N_A = 6.02 \times 10^{23}\) particles (the Avogadro constant). It is defined as the number of atoms in 12 g of carbon-12.

\( n = \dfrac{M}{M_m} \qquad N = n N_A = \dfrac{M N_A}{M_m} \)

where \(M\) is the mass of the sample, \(M_m\) is the molar mass, \(n\) is the number of moles, and \(N\) is the total number of molecules.

⬡ QUICK CHECK · Q4 · Short Answer
How many moles are contained in 1.6 kg of oxygen gas? (Molar mass of O₂ = 32 g mol⁻¹ = 0.032 kg mol⁻¹)
\(n = M/M_m = 1.6 / 0.032 = 50 \text{ mol}\)
05 The Ideal Gas Equation

Combining all three gas laws gives a single equation of state. An ideal gas obeys this exactly:

\( pV = nRT \qquad \text{or equivalently} \qquad pV = NkT \)

where \(R = 8.31 \text{ J mol}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1}\) is the universal molar gas constant, \(k = R/N_A = 1.38 \times 10^{-23} \text{ J K}^{-1}\) is the Boltzmann constant (gas constant per molecule), \(n\) is moles, and \(N\) is number of molecules.

The combined gas equation for a fixed amount of gas changing state:

\( \dfrac{p_1 V_1}{T_1} = \dfrac{p_2 V_2}{T_2} \)
UNITS CHECK \(p\) in Pa, \(V\) in m³, \(T\) in K. Units of \(pV\): Pa·m³ = N·m = J. This is why \(R\) carries J mol⁻¹ K⁻¹.

06 Molecular Kinetic Energy & Temperature

From kinetic theory, the pressure exerted by \(N\) molecules each of mass \(m\) with mean-square speed \(\overline{c^2}\) is:

\( pV = \tfrac{1}{3} N m \overline{c^2} \)

Setting this equal to \(nRT\) and using \(N/n = N_A\):

\( \tfrac{1}{2} m \overline{c^2} = \tfrac{3}{2} \dfrac{R}{N_A} T = \tfrac{3}{2} k T \)
KEY RESULT The mean translational kinetic energy of a molecule \(E = \tfrac{3}{2}kT\) is directly proportional to absolute temperature. Double the Kelvin temperature → double the mean KE. The internal energy of an ideal gas is the sum of all these kinetic energies.
⬡ QUICK CHECK · Q6 · Short Answer
Calculate the mean translational KE (in J) of an oxygen molecule at 20 °C. (\(k = 1.38 \times 10^{-23}\) J K⁻¹)
\(T = 293\text{ K},\quad E = \tfrac{3}{2}kT = 1.5 \times 1.38\times10^{-23} \times 293 \approx 6.07\times10^{-21}\text{ J}\)
⬡ QUICK CHECK · Q7 · Short Answer
A bicycle tyre contains air at 15 °C and 280 kPa. After riding, pressure rises to 290 kPa at constant volume. Calculate the ratio: internal energy at new temperature / internal energy at 15 °C.
Since \(U \propto T\) and \(p \propto T\) (constant V): \(T_2/T_1 = p_2/p_1 = 290/280 \approx 1.036\). Ratio ≈ 1.036.
07 Distribution of Molecular Speeds

Gas molecules do not all move at the same speed. The Maxwell–Boltzmann distribution shows that the number of molecules peaks at a characteristic speed, with tails to very high and very low values. No molecules are stationary.

  • At higher temperature the curve becomes flatter and broader.
  • The peak shifts to higher speeds — more molecules are in the high-speed tail.
  • \(\overline{c^2} \propto T\), so root-mean-square speed \(c_\text{rms} = \sqrt{3kT/m}\).
