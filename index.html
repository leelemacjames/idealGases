01 The Kinetic Model of an Ideal Gas

Before deriving any equations, we need a physical picture. An ideal gas is a theoretical model in which we deliberately strip away molecular complexity and keep only the essentials.

The four core assumptions:

  • The gas consists of a very large number of identical point-like molecules in rapid, random motion. "Point-like" means the volume of each molecule is negligible compared to the volume of the container.
  • Collisions between molecules, and between molecules and container walls, are perfectly elastic — kinetic energy is conserved. No energy is lost to heat or deformation during a collision.
  • There are no intermolecular forces except during the brief instant of a collision. Between collisions, molecules travel in straight lines at constant speed.
  • The duration of each collision is negligible compared to the time between collisions.
WHY THESE ASSUMPTIONS MATTER Assumption 2 ensures kinetic energy is conserved in collisions. Assumption 3 is the crucial one: if there are no intermolecular attractive or repulsive forces between collisions, there is no potential energy associated with molecular separation. The total internal energy of the gas is therefore purely kinetic — it is just the sum of all the individual molecular kinetic energies. This is what makes the model tractable.
02 Moles, Avogadro & Counting Molecules

One mole of any substance contains exactly \(N_A = 6.02 \times 10^{23}\) entities (the Avogadro constant). This number is defined such that one mole of carbon-12 atoms has a mass of exactly 12 g.

\( n = \dfrac{M}{M_m} \qquad\qquad N = n N_A \)

where \(M\) is the total mass of the sample, \(M_m\) is the molar mass (mass per mole, in kg mol⁻¹ or g mol⁻¹), \(n\) is the number of moles, and \(N\) is the total number of molecules. The Boltzmann constant is simply the gas constant divided by Avogadro's number:

\( k = \dfrac{R}{N_A} = \dfrac{8.31}{6.02 \times 10^{23}} \approx 1.38 \times 10^{-23} \text{ J K}^{-1} \)

It is the gas constant per molecule rather than per mole. You will see both \(nR\) and \(Nk\) in gas equations — they are exactly the same quantity.

⬡ CHECK · Q1 · Short Answer
How many molecules are contained in 3.2 g of oxygen gas O₂? (Molar mass of O₂ = 32 g mol⁻¹, \(N_A = 6.02 \times 10^{23}\) mol⁻¹)
\(n = 3.2/32 = 0.1\text{ mol}\). Then \(N = nN_A = 0.1 \times 6.02\times10^{23} = 6.02\times10^{22}\text{ molecules}\).
03 What is the Root-Mean-Square Speed?

Gas molecules do not all move at the same speed — they have a continuous distribution of speeds. To describe the typical speed, we need a single representative number. There are three natural candidates, and they are not the same.

Consider five molecules with speeds (in m s⁻¹): 200, 300, 400, 500, 600.

Name Definition Formula applied Value
Arithmetic mean \(\bar{c}\) Average of speeds \(\dfrac{200+300+400+500+600}{5}\) 400 m s⁻¹
Geometric mean Fifth root of product \((200\times300\times400\times500\times600)^{1/5}\) ≈ 383 m s⁻¹
RMS speed \(c_\text{rms}\) Square root of mean of squared speeds \(\sqrt{\dfrac{200^2+300^2+400^2+500^2+600^2}{5}}\) ≈ 412 m s⁻¹

The RMS is always larger than the arithmetic mean for any spread of positive values — the squaring step weights faster molecules more heavily. In physics, we use the RMS because kinetic energy goes as \(v^2\): when we write \(\frac{1}{2}m\overline{c^2}\), we literally need the mean of the squared speeds, not the square of the mean speed. These are different quantities:

\( \overline{c^2} \neq \left(\bar{c}\right)^2 \qquad \Longrightarrow \qquad c_\text{rms} = \sqrt{\overline{c^2}} \neq \bar{c} \)
WHY NOT JUST USE THE ARITHMETIC MEAN? Because kinetic energy is \(\tfrac{1}{2}mv^2\), the mean kinetic energy of the gas involves \(\overline{v^2}\), not \(\bar{v}\). Using \(\bar{v}\) would give the wrong answer for energy calculations. The RMS is the natural average when the quantity of interest is proportional to the square of what you're averaging.
⊕ WORKED EXAMPLE — RMS calculation
Three molecules have speeds 100, 200, and 300 m s⁻¹. Find \(c_\text{rms}\).

Step 1 — square each speed: \(100^2 = 10\,000\), \(200^2 = 40\,000\), \(300^2 = 90\,000\).

Step 2 — take the mean: \(\overline{c^2} = (10\,000 + 40\,000 + 90\,000)/3 = 140\,000/3 \approx 46\,667\text{ m}^2\text{s}^{-2}\).

Step 3 — take the square root: \(c_\text{rms} = \sqrt{46\,667} \approx 216\text{ m s}^{-1}\).

Compare: arithmetic mean = \((100+200+300)/3 = 200\text{ m s}^{-1}\). As expected, \(c_\text{rms} > \bar{c}\).

⬡ CHECK · Q2 · Short Answer
Four molecules have speeds 100, 200, 300, and 400 m s⁻¹. Calculate \(c_\text{rms}\) to 3 significant figures.
\(\overline{c^2} = \dfrac{100^2+200^2+300^2+400^2}{4} = \dfrac{10000+40000+90000+160000}{4} = \dfrac{300000}{4} = 75000\). Then \(c_\text{rms} = \sqrt{75000} \approx 274\text{ m s}^{-1}\).
04 Deriving \(pV = \tfrac{1}{3}Nm\overline{c^2}\) — Pressure from Kinetic Theory

We now derive the central result of kinetic theory. The strategy is to calculate the force exerted on a wall by a single molecule, then sum over all \(N\) molecules. The derivation uses Newton's second law and makes the isotropy assumption (motion is equally likely in all directions).

▸ DERIVATION — step by step
Step 1 Consider one molecule of mass \(m\) moving with speed component \(u\) towards a wall of area \(A\), in a cubic box of side \(L\).
Step 2 Elastic collision with the wall: momentum changes from \(+mu\) to \(-mu\). Change in momentum: \(\Delta p = 2mu\).
Step 3 Time between successive collisions with the same wall: the molecule travels \(2L\) at speed \(u\), so \(\Delta t = 2L/u\).
Step 4 Force from this one molecule on the wall: \(F = \Delta p / \Delta t = 2mu \div (2L/u) = mu^2/L\).
Step 5 Sum over all \(N\) molecules (each has a different \(x\)-component \(u_i\)): \[F_\text{total} = \dfrac{m}{L}\sum_{i=1}^{N} u_i^2 = \dfrac{Nm}{L}\overline{u^2}\]
where \(\overline{u^2}\) is the mean of the squared \(x\)-components of velocity.
Step 6 Isotropy: since motion is random, there is no preferred direction. Therefore \(\overline{u^2} = \overline{v^2} = \overline{w^2}\) (for \(x\), \(y\), \(z\) components). Also \(\overline{c^2} = \overline{u^2} + \overline{v^2} + \overline{w^2} = 3\overline{u^2}\), so \(\overline{u^2} = \tfrac{1}{3}\overline{c^2}\).
Step 7 Pressure \(p = F/A = F/L^2\) and volume \(V = L^3\): \[p = \dfrac{Nm\overline{u^2}}{L^3} = \dfrac{Nm \cdot \tfrac{1}{3}\overline{c^2}}{V}\]
\( pV = \tfrac{1}{3}Nm\overline{c^2} \)

A quick dimensional check: \([N] = 1\), \([m] = \text{kg}\), \([\overline{c^2}] = \text{m}^2\text{s}^{-2}\). So \([Nm\overline{c^2}] = \text{kg m}^2\text{s}^{-2} = \text{J}\). And \([pV] = \text{Pa}\cdot\text{m}^3 = \text{N m}^{-2}\cdot\text{m}^3 = \text{N m} = \text{J}\). ✓

⬡ CHECK · Q3 · Multiple Choice
In the derivation above, the step \(\overline{u^2} = \tfrac{1}{3}\overline{c^2}\) relies on which assumption?
ACollisions with the wall are perfectly elastic.
BThe number of molecules is very large.
CMolecular motion is random and isotropic — no preferred direction of travel.
DThere are no intermolecular forces between collisions.
C is correct. The isotropy assumption says all three Cartesian components of velocity are statistically equivalent: \(\overline{u^2} = \overline{v^2} = \overline{w^2}\). Since \(\overline{c^2} = \overline{u^2}+\overline{v^2}+\overline{w^2} = 3\overline{u^2}\), we get \(\overline{u^2} = \frac{1}{3}\overline{c^2}\). Option A ensures kinetic energy is conserved but says nothing about direction; options B and D are other assumptions but do not produce this particular step.
05 Mean Kinetic Energy and Temperature: \(E_k = \tfrac{3}{2}kT\)

We now connect kinetic theory to thermodynamics by combining the result of Section 04 with the ideal gas equation.

▸ DERIVATION — linking \(pV = \tfrac{1}{3}Nm\overline{c^2}\) to \(pV = NkT\)
Step 1 From kinetic theory: \(\quad pV = \tfrac{1}{3}Nm\overline{c^2}\)
Step 2 From thermodynamics (the ideal gas law): \(\quad pV = NkT\)
Step 3 Set equal: \(\quad \tfrac{1}{3}Nm\overline{c^2} = NkT\)
Step 4 Divide both sides by \(N\): \(\quad \tfrac{1}{3}m\overline{c^2} = kT\)
Step 5 Multiply both sides by \(\tfrac{3}{2}\): \(\quad \tfrac{1}{2}m\overline{c^2} = \tfrac{3}{2}kT\)
\( E_k = \tfrac{1}{2}m\overline{c^2} = \tfrac{3}{2}kT \)

The left side is precisely the mean translational kinetic energy per molecule. The \(\tfrac{1}{2}m\overline{c^2}\) uses the mean of the squared speeds, exactly as we defined for the RMS — not the arithmetic mean, not the geometric mean. This is why the RMS speed is the natural choice.

THREE IMPORTANT CONSEQUENCES (i) Mean KE depends only on temperature — not on the identity of the gas (mass, type, etc.). A helium atom and a nitrogen molecule at the same temperature have the same mean kinetic energy. (ii) Since internal energy \(U = N \cdot \tfrac{3}{2}kT = \tfrac{3}{2}nRT\), it depends only on \(n\) and \(T\). (iii) At absolute zero (\(T = 0\text{ K}\)), \(E_k = 0\) — molecules have no thermal kinetic energy. This gives physical meaning to 0 K as the true zero of the temperature scale.

From \(E_k = \tfrac{3}{2}kT\) and \(E_k = \tfrac{1}{2}m\overline{c^2}\), we can also write the RMS speed explicitly:

\( c_\text{rms} = \sqrt{\overline{c^2}} = \sqrt{\dfrac{3kT}{m}} = \sqrt{\dfrac{3RT}{M_m}} \)
⊕ WORKED EXAMPLE — Mean KE
Find the mean translational kinetic energy of a gas molecule at 27 °C.

Convert: \(T = 27 + 273 = 300\text{ K}\).

\(E_k = \tfrac{3}{2}kT = \tfrac{3}{2} \times 1.38\times10^{-23} \times 300 = 6.21\times10^{-21}\text{ J}\).

Note: this answer is the same regardless of which gas — hydrogen, oxygen, xenon, anything.

⬡ CHECK · Q4 · Short Answer
Calculate the rms speed of a nitrogen molecule (N₂, molar mass 0.028 kg mol⁻¹) at 300 K. (\(R = 8.31\) J mol⁻¹ K⁻¹)
\(c_\text{rms} = \sqrt{3RT/M_m} = \sqrt{3 \times 8.31 \times 300 / 0.028} = \sqrt{266\,464} \approx 516\text{ m s}^{-1}\).
06 The Gas Laws & the Ideal Gas Equation

The three empirical gas laws are special cases of the ideal gas equation, each obtained by holding one variable constant.

\(\underbrace{pV = nRT}_{\text{molar form}} \qquad\equiv\qquad \underbrace{pV = NkT}_{\text{molecular form}}\)

where \(R = 8.31\text{ J mol}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1}\), \(k = 1.38\times10^{-23}\text{ J K}^{-1}\), \(n\) = moles, \(N\) = number of molecules. Temperature must always be in Kelvin: \(T(\text{K}) = T(°\text{C}) + 273\).

Law Held constant Relationship Form
Boyle's \(T\), \(n\) \(p \propto 1/V\) \(p_1V_1 = p_2V_2\)
Charles' \(p\), \(n\) \(V \propto T\) \(V_1/T_1 = V_2/T_2\)
Pressure law \(V\), \(n\) \(p \propto T\) \(p_1/T_1 = p_2/T_2\)
Combined \(n\) only — \(\dfrac{p_1V_1}{T_1} = \dfrac{p_2V_2}{T_2}\)
UNITS CHECK — pV = nRT \(p\) in Pa = N m⁻², \(V\) in m³, so \(pV\) has units N m = J. On the right: \(n\) in mol, \(T\) in K, so \(R\) must have units J mol⁻¹ K⁻¹. This is why you cannot use cm³ or kPa directly — convert everything to SI first.
⊕ WORKED EXAMPLE — Boyle's Law
A gas is compressed at constant temperature from 4.0 × 10⁻³ m³ at 1.0 × 10⁵ Pa to a new pressure of 4.0 × 10⁵ Pa. Find the new volume.

Using \(p_1V_1 = p_2V_2\):

\(V_2 = \dfrac{p_1 V_1}{p_2} = \dfrac{1.0\times10^5 \times 4.0\times10^{-3}}{4.0\times10^5} = 1.0\times10^{-3}\text{ m}^3\).

The volume decreases by a factor of 4, as expected — pressure quadrupled.

⬡ CHECK · Q5 · Multiple Choice
A fixed mass of gas at 27 °C occupies 2.0 × 10⁻³ m³ at 1.0 × 10⁵ Pa. What volume does it occupy at 127 °C and 2.0 × 10⁵ Pa?
A4.0 × 10⁻³ m³
B1.33 × 10⁻³ m³
C2.67 × 10⁻³ m³
D1.0 × 10⁻³ m³
B is correct. Convert temperatures: \(T_1 = 300\text{ K}\), \(T_2 = 400\text{ K}\). Using the combined gas law: \(V_2 = V_1 \times \dfrac{p_1}{p_2} \times \dfrac{T_2}{T_1} = 2.0\times10^{-3} \times \dfrac{10^5}{2\times10^5} \times \dfrac{400}{300} = 2.0\times10^{-3} \times 0.5 \times 1.333 = 1.33\times10^{-3}\text{ m}^3\). Option A ignores both the pressure change and the temperature conversion. Option C only accounts for temperature. Watch out for the Kelvin conversion — adding 100 K to 27 °C gives 400 K, not 227 K.
⬡ CHECK · Q6 · Short Answer
A gas occupies volume \(V\) at 27 °C and constant pressure. At what temperature (in °C) will the volume be \(3V\)?
\(T_1 = 300\text{ K}\). Charles' law: \(V_1/T_1 = V_2/T_2\), so \(T_2 = T_1 \times V_2/V_1 = 300 \times 3 = 900\text{ K} = 627°\text{C}\).
07 The Maxwell–Boltzmann Speed Distribution

The equation \(c_\text{rms} = \sqrt{3kT/m}\) gives a single representative speed, but real gases contain molecules with a wide spread of speeds. The Maxwell–Boltzmann distribution describes how many molecules have each speed.

  • The distribution is asymmetric: it has a long tail to high speeds but cuts off sharply near zero (no molecules are stationary).
  • There are three characteristic speeds: the most probable speed (peak of curve) < mean speed < rms speed. All three are different.
  • At higher temperature: the peak shifts to higher speeds, the curve broadens and flattens, and the area under the curve (total number of molecules) stays constant.
  • Since \(c_\text{rms} \propto \sqrt{T/m}\), lighter molecules (smaller \(m\)) move faster at the same temperature — this is why hydrogen escapes Earth's atmosphere while nitrogen does not.
⬡ CHECK · Q7 · Multiple Choice
A sample of gas is heated from 300 K to 1200 K at constant volume. By what factor does the rms speed of the molecules change?
A4
B16
C2
D√2
C is correct. Since \(c_\text{rms} \propto \sqrt{T}\), the ratio is \(\sqrt{T_2/T_1} = \sqrt{1200/300} = \sqrt{4} = 2\). A common error is to say the factor is 4 (confusing the ratio of temperatures with the ratio of speeds) or to forget the square root. Note: the mean KE would increase by a factor of 4, since \(E_k \propto T\) — the temperature ratio itself.
08 Extended Response Question
✦ EXTENDED RESPONSE · Q8
[8 marks]

A meteorological balloon is filled with helium (molar mass 4.0 × 10⁻³ kg mol⁻¹) at ground level where the temperature is 17 °C and pressure is 1.00 × 10⁵ Pa. The balloon rises until it reaches a region where the pressure is 2.50 × 10³ Pa and temperature is −43 °C.

  • (a) State the ideal gas equation and use it to find the ratio \(V_\text{final} / V_\text{initial}\). [3]
  • (b) Calculate the mean translational kinetic energy of a helium atom at ground level. [\(k = 1.38 \times 10^{-23}\) J K⁻¹] [2]
  • (c) Calculate the rms speed of a helium atom at the top of the flight. [\(R = 8.31\) J mol⁻¹ K⁻¹] [2]
  • (d) Explain, in terms of the kinetic model, why the balloon expands as it rises even though the helium atoms are moving more slowly at altitude. [1]

MODEL ANSWER

(a) The ideal gas equation is \(pV = nRT\). For a fixed amount of gas changing state, \(\dfrac{p_1 V_1}{T_1} = \dfrac{p_2 V_2}{T_2}\). Converting: \(T_1 = 290\text{ K}\), \(T_2 = 230\text{ K}\). Rearranging: \(\dfrac{V_2}{V_1} = \dfrac{p_1 T_2}{p_2 T_1} = \dfrac{1.00\times10^5 \times 230}{2.50\times10^3 \times 290} \approx 31.7\). The volume increases by a factor of approximately 32.

(b) \(T_1 = 290\text{ K}\). Mean KE \(= \tfrac{3}{2}kT = 1.5 \times 1.38\times10^{-23} \times 290 = 6.00\times10^{-21}\text{ J}\).

(c) At altitude, \(T_2 = 230\text{ K}\). \(c_\text{rms} = \sqrt{3RT/M_m} = \sqrt{3 \times 8.31 \times 230 / (4.0\times10^{-3})} = \sqrt{1\,433\,325} \approx 1197\text{ m s}^{-1} \approx 1.20\text{ km s}^{-1}\).

(d) Pressure is caused by molecules colliding with the container walls. At altitude, the external air pressure is much lower — the atmosphere exerts less inward force on the balloon skin. Even though the helium molecules are moving more slowly (lower temperature → lower mean KE), the reduced external pressure means the balloon expands until the pressure of the helium inside equals the lower external pressure. The rate of momentum transfer per unit area (internal pressure) drops as the volume increases, reaching equilibrium at a larger volume.
